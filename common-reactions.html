Common Reactions by Starting Material

A practical transformation map built around the CommonOrganicChemistry.com reaction index: first identify the functional group, then choose the product class, then check reagent fit, chemoselectivity, and scale-up risk.

Decision 1: Functional groupClassify substrate: alcohol, carbonyl, ester/acid, amine/amide/nitrile, nitro, alkene/alkyne, aryl halide, or protected intermediate.
Decision 2: Product classSelect the product family: oxidation level, substitution product, reduction product, C-C bond, C-N bond, or protecting group.
Decision 3: CompatibilityCheck compatibility: water, acid/base sensitivity, over-reduction, over-oxidation, beta-elimination, catalyst poison, and purification load.
Decision 4: PracticalityFor synthesis planning, prefer transformations that are mild, selective, operationally simple, and generate impurities that can be purged.

Alcohol Oxidation and Leaving Group Strategy
Alcohol -> carbonyl, acid, halide, azide, sulfonate
Alcohol as a synthesis hub
Alcohols are common intermediates because they can be oxidized, reduced indirectly, esterified, protected, or converted into better leaving groups. The main choice is whether you need to change oxidation state or replace OH with another atom/group.
R2CHOH -> R2C=O | RCH2OH -> RCHO -> RCO2H | ROH -> ROMs/ROTs -> RNu

Carbonyl, Ester, Acid, Amide and Nitrile Interconversions
Oxidation level control
Carbonyl oxidation-state ladder
Carbonyl chemistry is usually an oxidation-level problem. Aldehydes and ketones reduce easily to alcohols; esters and acids require stronger hydrides; nitriles can behave like masked acids, amides, amines, or aldehydes depending on hydrolysis/reduction conditions.
RCO2R' <-> RCO2H -> RCOCl -> RCONHR' | RCN -> RCONH2 -> RCO2H | RCN -> RCH2NH2
FamilyCommon moveUseful detail
AldehydeTo alcohol, acid, difluoride, alkeneNaBH4 reduces; oxidants give acids; Wittig converts C=O to C=C.
KetoneTo alcohol or methyleneHydride gives secondary alcohol; Wolff-Kishner/Clemmensen remove C=O.
EsterTo acid, aldehyde, alcoholHydrolysis, DIBAL-H, and LiAlH4 separate the three common outcomes.
AcidTo ester, alcohol, acid chlorideAcid chloride formation activates acids for fast downstream acyl substitution.
AmideTo nitrile or amineDehydration vs reduction depends on reagent class.
NitrileTo amide, acid, amine, aldehyde, esterPartial hydrolysis, full hydrolysis, strong reduction, DIBAL-H, and alcoholysis are the common branches.
Route planning caution: Strong hydrides, acid chlorides, and hydrolysis conditions can destroy protecting groups or sensitive heterocycles. Check compatibility before choosing the shortest arrow.

Nitrogen Chemistry: Amines, Amides, Nitro and Reductive Amination
C-N bond formation and reduction
Nitrogen functional group playbook
Nitrogen groups are central in medicinal chemistry. The common operations are acylation of amines, amide coupling, nitro reduction, nitrile reduction, urea formation, and reductive amination from carbonyl compounds.
RNH2 + RCOX -> RCONHR | ArNO2 -> ArNH2 | R2C=O + R'NH2 + reductant -> R2CHNHR'
ReactionTypical conditionsCommon risk
Amine to amideAcid chloride/base, anhydride, HATU, EDCI, T3PSalt formation, over-acylation, poor coupling with hindered partners.
Nitro to amineFe/HCl, SnCl2, Zn/NH4Cl, H2/Pd-COther reducible groups may react under hydrogenation.

Alkenes, Alkynes and Substitution Handles
Addition, oxidation, reduction, SN2/SNAr
Unsaturated and leaving-group chemistry
Alkenes and alkynes are handles for hydrogenation, hydration, epoxidation, ozonolysis, and oxidative cleavage. Alkyl chlorides, bromides, iodides, mesylates, tosylates, and triflates are handles for substitution and elimination. Activated aryl halides can undergo SNAr when electron-withdrawing groups stabilize the Meisenheimer intermediate.
SubstrateCommon transformationPlanning note
AlkeneTo alkane, alcohol, aldehyde, epoxideChoose hydrogenation, hydroboration/oxymercuration, ozonolysis, or peracid conditions.
AlkyneTo alkane, cis/trans alkene, ketone/aldehydeLindlar gives cis alkene; dissolving metal gives trans alkene; hydration gives carbonyl.
Alkyl halide/sulfonateTo alcohol, ether, azide, nitrile, aminePrimary substrates favor SN2; tertiary substrates favor SN1/E1/E2.
Aryl fluoride/chloride/bromideSNAr substitutionBest with ortho/para nitro, cyano, carbonyl, or other electron-withdrawing groups.

Protection and Deprotection Map
Boc, Cbz, benzyl, THP, SEM, TBS, PMB, trityl, acetyl
Protecting group selection
Protecting groups are useful only when they survive the planned chemistry and can be removed without damaging the product. Select them backward from the deprotection step, not only forward from the starting material.
GroupCommon useRemoval logic
BocAmine protectionAcid labile; often removed with TFA or HCl/dioxane.
CbzAmine protectionHydrogenolysis; avoid reducible groups.
Benzyl/PMBAlcohol/phenol protectionHydrogenolysis or oxidative cleavage for PMB.
TBSAlcohol protectionFluoride or acid; sensitive to strong acid over time.
THPAlcohol protectionAcid labile; can generate diastereomeric mixtures.
TritylAlcohol/amine protectionBulky and acid labile; useful for selectivity.

Fast Route-Design Checklist

Chemoselectivity
Will the reagent attack only the intended functional group? Check carbonyls, nitro groups, alkenes, aryl halides, and protecting groups.
Oxidation level
Ask whether the product is more oxidized, more reduced, or simply substituted. This prevents choosing a reagent class too early.
Operational fit
Dry conditions, low temperature, microwave heating, hydrogenation, azides, and chromium reagents all change scale-up practicality.
Purification
Triphenylphosphine oxide, metal residues, inorganic salts, colored Pd residues, and polar amide byproducts may dominate the work-up.
Retrosynthesis
Use the map backward: product alcohol can come from carbonyl reduction or Grignard addition; biaryl can come from Suzuki; amine can come from nitro/nitrile/reductive amination.
Interview answer style
Name the functional group change, reagent, mechanism class, side reaction, and one purification/process concern.
